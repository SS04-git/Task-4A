LEGAL FRAMEWORK

CURRENT LAWS

Existing privacy laws in many jurisdictions were not designed with modern digital technologies, such as deepfakes, in mind. While laws surrounding defamation, copyright infringement, and privacy invasion may be applied to deepfake cases, these frameworks were created before the emergence of such technologies. As a result, they often fall short of addressing the specific nuances of digital impersonation and the unauthorized creation of content. This gap in legislation leaves victims with limited legal recourse and underscores the need for a more tailored approach to privacy and intellectual property protection in the context of AI-generated content.

This highlights the urgency of creating legal mechanisms that are better suited to address these new forms of harm.

PROPOSED LEGAL CHANGES

In response to the challenges posed by deepfake technology, legislative changes are being proposed to better protect individuals' rights and address the misuse of AI-generated content. One key proposal involves broadening the definition of personal data to include biometric information, which is frequently used in the creation of deepfakes. This would ensure that individuals' likenesses are better protected under privacy laws. Additionally, there is growing pressure on lawmakers to establish clearer guidelines for the ethical use of AI-generated content, ensuring a balance between innovation and personal privacy rights. International discussions around intellectual property also emphasize the need for clearer ownership guidelines when deepfakes involve copyrighted material. These proposed changes reflect a growing recognition of the need for comprehensive protections that specifically target the misuse of deepfake technology, as the risks to personal privacy and security continue to rise.
